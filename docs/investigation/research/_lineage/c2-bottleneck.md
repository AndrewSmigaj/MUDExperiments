# The Hand-Authored-Ontology / Commonsense-Knowledge Bottleneck

> **Thesis of this section.** For roughly half a century, the limiting factor on building a deep, "do-anything" world system — a simulation in which an agent or player can attempt *any* sensible action and get a sensible response — was not the simulation machinery. It was the **world knowledge** the machinery had to consult: the objects, their properties and affordances, the physical rules, and the appropriate responses. Every serious attempt to supply that knowledge ran into the same wall. **Authoring broad, deep, grounded commonsense knowledge by hand does not scale.** This is the "commonsense knowledge problem" / "knowledge-acquisition bottleneck," and it is the load-bearing reason deep world systems stayed narrow. What follows is a narrative of attempts and the walls they hit.

---

## 1. The symbolic-AI knowledge-representation era: "understanding needs a lot of hand-built knowledge"

The founding insight of 1970s AI knowledge representation was that intelligence is mostly *knowledge*, not inference: to understand even a simple sentence or scene, a system must already possess a large stock of structured background knowledge about how the world normally works.

**Minsky's frames (1974).** Marvin Minsky proposed *frames* in "A Framework for Representing Knowledge," issued as MIT AI Laboratory Memo 306 in June 1974 and reprinted in *The Psychology of Computer Vision* (ed. P. Winston, 1975) ([Wikipedia: Frame (AI)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frame_(artificial_intelligence)); [MIT memo PDF](https://courses.media.mit.edu/2004spring/mas966/Minsky%201974%20Framework%20for%20knowledge.pdf)). A frame is "a data-structure for representing a stereotyped situation, like being in a certain kind of living room, or going to a child's birthday party." Its upper levels encode things always true of the situation; its lower levels have "slots" that get filled with the specifics of the case at hand ([Wikipedia: Frame (AI)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frame_(artificial_intelligence))). Crucially, Minsky framed networks of linked frames as the way to represent "the enormous volume of background common sense knowledge required to understand even simple natural language texts" ([Wikipedia: Frame (AI)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frame_(artificial_intelligence))). The promise: stereotyped knowledge, hand-encoded once, lets a system fill in everything left unsaid.

**Schank & Abelson's scripts (1977).** Roger Schank and Robert Abelson pushed the same idea into language understanding in *Scripts, Plans, Goals and Understanding: An Inquiry into Human Knowledge Structures* (Lawrence Erlbaum, 1977) ([summary](http://www.jimdavies.org/summaries/schank1977-2.html)). A *script* is structured knowledge of a stereotyped event sequence — the canonical example being the **restaurant script** (enter, be seated, order, eat, pay, leave). Their Script Applier Mechanism (SAM) used the restaurant script to comprehend a terse narrative such as "I had lunch at a restaurant," inferring the unstated steps — ordering, eating, paying — that a human reader supplies automatically ([summary](http://www.jimdavies.org/summaries/schank1977-2.html)). Their thesis was explicit: "understanding is knowledge-based," and conventional situations are understood by retrieving highly structured knowledge one has effectively "been in before" ([summary](http://www.jimdavies.org/summaries/schank1977-2.html)).

**The dawning realization.** Frames and scripts worked beautifully on the situations someone had hand-built a frame or script for — and not at all otherwise. Each new domain, each new situation, demanded another hand-authored structure. This is where the field first felt the *scale* of the knowledge required: human understanding leans on a vast, mostly tacit body of everyday knowledge, and somebody would have to write all of it down. John McCarthy had already proposed, from the late 1950s, formalizing commonsense reasoning in first-order logic ("Programs with Common Sense," 1959), but doing so requires "a large amount of additional logical scaffolding to precisely define the terms used … and their interrelationships" ([Wikipedia: Symbolic AI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symbolic_artificial_intelligence)). The promise of hand-built knowledge collided, immediately, with its cost.

## 2. Cyc (Douglas Lenat, 1984–present): the poster child of the bottleneck

If one project embodies the bet that commonsense could simply be **hand-coded at scale**, it is Cyc. It is also the field's central cautionary tale.

**The bet.** Douglas Lenat launched Cyc in July 1984 at the Microelectronics and Computer Technology Corporation (MCC) in Austin, Texas, where he served as Principal Scientist from 1984 to 1994 ([Wikipedia: Cyc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyc); [Wikipedia: Douglas Lenat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Douglas_Lenat)). The name comes from en**cyc**lopedia. The premise: the reason AI programs were brittle was that they lacked the millions of pieces of ordinary "consensus reality" knowledge every human shares, so the fix was to assemble, by hand, "a comprehensive ontology and knowledge base that spans the basic concepts and rules about how the world works" ([Wikipedia: Cyc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyc); [Britannica: CYC](https://www.britannica.com/topic/CYC)). Knowledge would be entered as logical assertions in a formal language (CycL), line by line, by trained "ontological engineers."

**The projected scale.** Lenat understood from the outset that this was an enormous undertaking. In 1986 he estimated Cyc would require **at least 250,000 rules and on the order of 1,000 person-years of effort — "probably twice that"** ([Wikipedia: Douglas Lenat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Douglas_Lenat)). His working intuition was that human common sense might amount to something on the order of **~100 million assertions** that would need to be encoded ([Encyclopedia: Cyc](https://encyclopedia.pub/entry/30118)). This was a deliberate, decades-long, industrial-scale knowledge-engineering bet.

**The scale actually reached.** Over three decades Cyc grew large — but never to "all of common sense":

- **1994:** roughly **100,000 terms** in the ontology and about **1 million assertions** in the knowledge base ([Wikipedia: Cyc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyc)).
- **2002:** the project had consumed about **$60 million and 600 person-years** of effort from "programmers, philosophers and others" ([Wikipedia: Cyc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyc)).
- **2017:** roughly **1,500,000 terms** and about **24.5 million assertions**, with the team having invested **about 2,000 person-years** (roughly 20 person-centuries) of hand-authoring ([Wikipedia: Cyc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyc); [Wikipedia: Douglas Lenat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Douglas_Lenat)).

Lenat founded Cycorp, Inc. in January 1995 to continue the work commercially, serving as CEO until his death in 2023 ([Wikipedia: Cyc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyc); [Wolfram, "Remembering Doug Lenat"](https://writings.stephenwolfram.com/2023/09/remembering-doug-lenat-1950-2023-and-his-quest-to-capture-the-world-with-logic/)). Public subsets were released — **OpenCyc** (spring 2002: ~6,000 concepts, ~60,000 facts; OpenCyc 4.0 in June 2012: ~239,000 concepts and ~2,093,000 facts) and **ResearchCyc** (July 2006) — with OpenCyc discontinued around March 2017 ([Wikipedia: Cyc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyc)).

**The outcome and the assessment.** After ~40 years and thousands of person-years, Cyc did not deliver general AI or robust open-domain common sense. The widely shared assessment is that hand-authoring commonsense at world scale is intractable. Machine-learning researcher Pedro Domingos called Cyc "a catastrophic failure," citing its unending appetite for hand-entered data and its inability to learn or self-evolve ([Wikipedia: Cyc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyc)). Stephen Wolfram, sympathetic to Lenat, eulogized Cyc as "a magnificent experiment — that if nothing else ultimately served to demonstrate the importance of building frameworks beyond logic alone," and observed that large language models later achieved comparable commonsense behavior through pattern recognition rather than explicit hand-coded logic ([Wolfram, "Remembering Doug Lenat"](https://writings.stephenwolfram.com/2023/09/remembering-doug-lenat-1950-2023-and-his-quest-to-capture-the-world-with-logic/)). The honest summary: the bottleneck was real, Cyc paid its full price, and the price of *complete* broad commonsense coverage proved effectively unpayable by hand.

## 3. Naive & qualitative physics: hand-encoding physical commonsense — and staying small

A parallel effort tried to formalize a specific, tractable-looking slice of common sense: **everyday physical knowledge** (objects, liquids, containment, heat, support, motion). It is instructive because it shows the bottleneck even within a "narrow" domain.

**Hayes's Naive Physics Manifesto (1978/1979/1985).** Pat Hayes issued an explicit call to formalize the whole of naive physical knowledge by hand. The *Naive Physics Manifesto* circulated as a 1978 working paper (University of Essex) and appeared in 1979 in *Expert Systems in the Microelectronic Age* (ed. D. Michie, Edinburgh University Press); he followed it with *The Second Naive Physics Manifesto* in 1985, in *Formal Theories of the Commonsense World* (eds. J. Hobbs and R. Moore) ([Wikipedia: Pat Hayes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pat_Hayes)). Hayes proposed a *large-scale* project to axiomatize "the entire knowledge of physics of naive reasoners" in declarative symbolic form, organized into clusters of interconnected concepts ([Davis, "Naive Physics Perplex," AI Magazine 1998](https://ojs.aaai.org//index.php/aimagazine/article/view/1424)). The manifesto inspired a generation of work in commonsense formalization and ontology ([Wikipedia: Pat Hayes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pat_Hayes)).

**Qualitative physics (1984).** Rather than tackle "all of physics," the field's most productive line built *qualitative* models of physical processes — reasoning about increase/decrease and inequalities instead of exact numbers. Two foundational papers appeared together in *Artificial Intelligence* volume 24 (1984): Kenneth Forbus's **Qualitative Process Theory**, which represents how processes (flow, heating, boiling, stretching) drive change over time using a "quantity space" of inequalities (Forbus, *Artificial Intelligence* 24 (1984): 85–168) ([QPT PDF](https://www.qrg.northwestern.edu/papers/Files/QPT-PHD(searchable).pdf)); and Johan de Kleer & John Seely Brown's **"A Qualitative Physics Based on Confluences,"** which models physical devices via a "device ontology" (de Kleer & Brown, *Artificial Intelligence* 24 (1984)) ([Northwestern QR review](https://www.qrg.northwestern.edu/papers/Files/crc7.pdf)). The most-used ontologies in the field became precisely these two: the **device** ontology (de Kleer & Brown) and the **process** ontology (Forbus) ([Northwestern QR review](https://www.qrg.northwestern.edu/papers/Files/crc7.pdf)).

**Why it stayed small.** Despite real elegance, naive/qualitative physics never produced a broad, general theory of everyday physical common sense. In his retrospective "The Naive Physics Perplex" (*AI Magazine* 19(4), 1998: 51–79), Ernest Davis observes that the field had largely abandoned Hayes's grand unified ambition in favor of **"microworlds," each covering only a small range of physical phenomena**, with the goal merely of a competence theory adequate to justify inferences within that narrow slice ([Davis, "Naive Physics Perplex"](https://ojs.aaai.org//index.php/aimagazine/article/view/1424)). Axiomatizing liquids, or containment, or heat *thoroughly enough to be correct and general* turned out to be enormous, and the pieces did not compose cheaply into a whole. The same wall as Cyc, in miniature: depth and breadth and correctness, all at once, are too expensive to author by hand — even for "just" physics.

## 4. Lexical & commonsense KBs as a progression of strategies: hand-code → harvest → crowdsource

The decades after Cyc can be read as a search for a *cheaper way to acquire the same knowledge*. Each strategy lowered the per-fact cost — and each still produced resources that were sparse, noisy, and incomplete relative to "the whole world."

**Strategy 0 — expert lexical hand-curation: WordNet (1985→).** Psychologist George A. Miller and colleagues began WordNet at Princeton in 1985: a hand-built lexical database in which nouns, verbs, adjectives, and adverbs are grouped into **synsets** (sets of synonyms denoting one concept) and linked by semantic relations such as hypernymy/hyponymy ([Wikipedia: WordNet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WordNet); [History of Information](https://www.historyofinformation.com/detail.php?entryid=2471)). Funded initially by the U.S. Office of Naval Research and later DARPA and NSF, and later directed by Christiane Fellbaum, WordNet grew to **155,327 words in 175,979 synsets** (207,016 word-sense pairs) ([Wikipedia: WordNet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WordNet)). WordNet was a triumph — but note what it is and isn't: it captures *lexical/taxonomic* relations (a robin is a bird), not the grounded, situational commonsense of what things *do* or how they *behave*. Expert hand-curation could cover the dictionary; it could not cover the world.

**Strategy 1 — crowd-harvest: Open Mind Common Sense / ConceptNet (1999→).** To escape the cost of expert engineers, MIT's Open Mind Common Sense (OMCS) — launched in 1999 by Marvin Minsky, Push Singh, Catherine Havasi, and others — opened knowledge collection to the public, letting ordinary web volunteers type in commonsense facts ([Wikipedia: OMCS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_Mind_Common_Sense); [MIT Media Lab](https://www.media.mit.edu/projects/conceptnet-new/overview/)). Active from 1999 to 2016, OMCS gathered **over 1 million English facts from more than 15,000 contributors** ([Wikipedia: OMCS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_Mind_Common_Sense)). Its semantic-network form, **ConceptNet**, became a widely used commonsense graph; by ConceptNet 5.5 (Speer, Chin & Havasi, AAAI 2017) it held **over 21 million edges and over 8 million nodes** across many languages (~1.5M English nodes) ([ConceptNet 5.5, arXiv 1612.03975](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1612.03975)). Far cheaper per fact than Cyc — but the trade was quality and coherence: crowd-harvested assertions are noisy, redundant, shallowly stated in free text, and unevenly distributed, covering whatever volunteers happened to think to type.

**Strategy 2 — templated crowdsourcing of inferential commonsense: ATOMIC (2019).** Sap et al.'s **ATOMIC: An Atlas of Machine Commonsense for If-Then Reasoning** (AAAI 2019) targeted the *inferential* "if-then" knowledge that WordNet and ConceptNet largely lacked — causes, effects, intents, reactions ([ATOMIC, dblp/AAAI 2019](https://dblp.org/rec/conf/aaai/SapBABLRRSC19.html)). Using structured crowdsourcing templates, ATOMIC collected about **300,000 event nodes** and roughly **877,000 "if-event-then" triples** across nine relation types, validated entirely by crowd workers ([survey, arXiv 1904.01172](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1904.01172)). It was deliberately *not* taxonomic/ontological but inferential ([survey, arXiv 1904.01172](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1904.01172)). It also marks the pivot point: ATOMIC was promptly used to *train* a neural model, COMET (Bosselut et al., 2019), to *generate* commonsense tuples rather than enumerate them by hand — an admission that hand/crowd enumeration could never reach full coverage ([COMET, arXiv 1906.05317](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1906.05317)).

**The progression, and the constant.** Hand-code (Cyc) → crowd-harvest (OMCS/ConceptNet) → templated crowdsource (ATOMIC): each step cut the cost per assertion by orders of magnitude. Yet every resulting resource remained **sparse, noisy, and incomplete** relative to a complete, grounded world model. As the survey literature puts it, the field faces a fundamental tension between coverage and quality — automated/harvested construction yields far larger resources than ConceptNet, but "of moderately lower quality," while hand-curated resources stay small ([Wikipedia: Commonsense knowledge (AI)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commonsense_knowledge_(artificial_intelligence))). None of these strategies produced a *complete, grounded* world ontology of objects, properties, affordances, and physical responses.

## 5. The explicit bottleneck synthesis

Pull the threads together. For about fifty years — from frames and scripts in the mid-1970s, through Cyc and naive physics in the 1980s, to ConceptNet and ATOMIC in the 2000s–2010s — the binding constraint on "a system that can do anything sensible in a modeled world" was **not** the inference engine, the simulator, or the parser. It was the **cost and incompleteness of authoring the world knowledge itself**: the objects, their properties and affordances, the physical rules, and the situationally appropriate responses.

This constraint has two long-standing names, and both apply:

- **The knowledge-acquisition bottleneck** (Edward Feigenbaum, from the 1970s; canonically "Knowledge Acquisition: The Bottleneck," 1982). Building knowledge-based systems is gated by the slow, "painstaking" manual process of eliciting expert knowledge and translating it into machine-usable form — so much so that it became known as "Feigenbaum's bottleneck" ([Stanford, Feigenbaum Papers: "Knowledge Acquisition: The Bottleneck"](https://exhibits.stanford.edu/feigenbaum/catalog/sq764cf8300)). The bottleneck is fundamentally about labor: hand-transfer of knowledge does not scale.

- **The commonsense knowledge problem** (after John McCarthy's program of formalizing common sense). Davis & Marcus's authoritative survey, "Commonsense Reasoning and Commonsense Knowledge in Artificial Intelligence" (*Communications of the ACM* 58(9), 2015), frames why progress has been so slow: commonsense knowledge is **both broad and deep**, hand-authored knowledge bases are inevitably incomplete and sparse in coverage, and scaling them to robustness carries near-prohibitive cost — which is exactly why Cyc's purely manual encoding could not reach the comprehensive coverage robust commonsense reasoning requires ([Davis & Marcus, CACM 2015, PDF](https://cs.nyu.edu/~davise/papers/CommonsenseFinal.pdf); [DOI 10.1145/2701413](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/2701413)). The Wikipedia framing calls the commonsense knowledge problem an unsolved, possibly "AI-complete" challenge, and notes that the expert-driven hand-coding tradition (CYC, WordNet) is precisely what makes it "labor-intensive" ([Wikipedia: Commonsense knowledge (AI)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commonsense_knowledge_(artificial_intelligence))).

Every approach hit the *same* wall from a different angle. The wall is the product **breadth × depth × grounding**: you need *many* concepts (breadth), each modeled *richly enough to act on* (depth), *connected to the physical/causal world* well enough to be correct (grounding) — and that product is too expensive to author by hand, whether the hand belongs to an ontologist (Cyc), a physicist-logician (naive physics), a dictionary-maker (WordNet), or a crowd (ConceptNet, ATOMIC). Lowering the cost per fact (harvesting, crowdsourcing) bought *more* facts but not *complete, grounded* coverage; raising the rigor (Cyc, naive physics) bought correctness but not breadth. No point on that trade-off curve yielded a complete, grounded world ontology by hand.

## Summary — the bottleneck argument, stated crisply

- **The constraint, not the engine.** For ~50 years the limiter on deep "do-anything" world systems was the **cost and incompleteness of authoring world knowledge** (objects, properties, affordances, physical rules, responses) — not the simulator or reasoner that consumed it ([Davis & Marcus, CACM 2015](https://cs.nyu.edu/~davise/papers/CommonsenseFinal.pdf)).

- **It has two canonical names.** The **knowledge-acquisition bottleneck** (Feigenbaum, 1970s–80s) and the **commonsense knowledge problem** (McCarthy; surveyed by Davis & Marcus) are the same wall seen from labor-cost and knowledge-coverage angles respectively ([Stanford/Feigenbaum](https://exhibits.stanford.edu/feigenbaum/catalog/sq764cf8300); [Wikipedia: Commonsense knowledge (AI)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commonsense_knowledge_(artificial_intelligence))).

- **Cyc is the definitive cautionary tale.** Begun in 1984, it absorbed ~2,000 person-years to reach ~24.5M assertions over ~1.5M terms by 2017 — vast, yet still not robust open-domain common sense, and judged by many a "catastrophic failure" of pure hand-coding ([Wikipedia: Cyc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyc); [Wikipedia: Douglas Lenat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Douglas_Lenat)).

- **Cheaper acquisition didn't dissolve the wall.** The hand-code → crowd-harvest → crowdsource progression (Cyc → OMCS/ConceptNet's 21M edges → ATOMIC's 877K if-then triples) cut cost per fact dramatically but still produced sparse, noisy, incomplete knowledge — none a complete grounded ontology ([ConceptNet 5.5](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1612.03975); [ATOMIC, AAAI 2019](https://dblp.org/rec/conf/aaai/SapBABLRRSC19.html)).

- **The wall is breadth × depth × grounding.** Rigor without breadth (Cyc, naive physics) or breadth without rigor/grounding (crowd KBs) — no hand-authoring strategy bought all three at once at world scale ([Davis, "Naive Physics Perplex," 1998](https://ojs.aaai.org//index.php/aimagazine/article/view/1424)).

- **The honest nuance.** The wall was specifically **broad, grounded commonsense/ontology coverage** — *not* any single domain. Hand-authoring succeeded handsomely in bounded slices: WordNet's lexicon, the restaurant script, qualitative-physics microworlds, Cyc within curated topics. What never closed was the *open-ended whole* — the "anything sensible, anywhere in the world" coverage a true do-anything world system would need ([Davis & Marcus, CACM 2015](https://cs.nyu.edu/~davise/papers/CommonsenseFinal.pdf); [Davis, "Naive Physics Perplex"](https://ojs.aaai.org//index.php/aimagazine/article/view/1424)).
